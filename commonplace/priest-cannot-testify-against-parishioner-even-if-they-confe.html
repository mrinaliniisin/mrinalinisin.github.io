Priest cannot testify against parishioner even if they confessed to a crime such as homicide in USA and India

In the USA, a confession of murder made to a priest during the sacrament of Reconciliation is generally not admissible in court, under the “priest–penitent privilege” recognised in all 50 states (and many other countries) to protect the confidentiality of communications between clergy and those seeking spiritual counsel. Under Canon Law the “Seal of Confession” is absolute and inviolable — a priest who reveals anything from a confession faces automatic excommunication, and priests are expected to go to prison rather than break it. The privilege applies only to communication meant to be confidential for spiritual guidance; some jurisdictions carve out exceptions (e.g. mandatory reporting of child abuse), and a statement made informally outside the sacrament is generally not protected.

In India, such a confession is also generally inadmissible, but for a different reason: it is treated as an extra-judicial confession — considered weak evidence that requires independent corroboration. Under the Indian Evidence Act a confession is admissible only if voluntary, and courts regard extra-judicial confessions as unreliable, requiring corroborating evidence before relying on one.
